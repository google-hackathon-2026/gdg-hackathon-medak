# 🃏 Proof Stories — Quick Reference Card

*Cheat sheet for Q&A. When a judge asks "Is this real?" — you read them a card.*

---

## Story 1: Mary Shufeldt — San Diego, USA, 1986

**What happened:** Jay Shufeldt found his wife Mary, 72, struggling to breathe. He called 911 three times over three hours using his TDD device for the deaf. Operators hung up every time, mistaking the tones for a prank. By the time his hearing daughter called by voice, Mary was dead.

**Why it matters for Medak:** This is the most documented case of a deaf person dying because emergency services couldn't handle non-voice communication. It happened in 1986 — and Serbia still has no alternative to voice-only 112 in 2026.

**Source:** Los Angeles Times, "A Deadly Silence," Aug 25, 1986; "Deaf Confront Deadly Problem," Feb 1, 1987.

---

## Story 2: William Wong — USA, 2010s

**What happened:** William Wong, who is deaf, had his gallbladder rupture while alone. He couldn't call 911, so he texted his daughter Gabriella. She didn't see the messages in time. He nearly died. His daughter went on to found AccesSOS, a nonprofit for deaf emergency access.

**Why it matters for Medak:** This is the origin story of AccesSOS — proof that the texting-a-friend workaround is a life-threatening gamble, not a solution.

**Source:** AccesSOS (accessos.io); AmeriDisability interview; Stand Together profile.

---

## Story 3: Andrew Jordan — London, UK, 2013

**What happened:** Andrew Jordan, deaf, and his deaf wife were hit by a drunk driver with two toddlers in the car. Not a single first responder — police, fire, or ambulance — could communicate with them. At the hospital, no sign language interpreter was arranged despite advance requests. A police officer gave him a *phone number* to arrange his own towing.

**Why it matters for Medak:** Even in a developed country with legal protections, the entire emergency chain failed a deaf family. Communication breakdown wasn't one moment — it was every moment.

**Source:** Deaf Magazine UK, "Deaf Man Fails to Get the Support He Needs Following Accident," Dec 29, 2013.

---

## Story 4: Magdiel Sanchez — Oklahoma City, USA, 2017

**What happened:** Police arrived at Magdiel Sanchez's home for a hit-and-run investigation. He was deaf and came out holding a metal pipe he used as a walking aid. Neighbors screamed "He can't hear you! He's deaf!" Officers ordered him to drop it. He couldn't hear them. They shot and killed him.

**Why it matters for Medak:** When emergency systems can't communicate with deaf people, the consequences aren't just delayed ambulances — they can be fatal encounters with responders themselves.

**Source:** Multiple national news outlets, 2017; Oklahoma City Police Department records.

---

## Story 5: The Cheshire Fire — UK

**What happened:** A child with two deaf parents called 999 to report a house fire but was too overwhelmed to speak clearly and hung up. The call was classified as a hoax. No fire engine was sent. The deaf father had to contact the fire station directly to get help.

**Why it matters for Medak:** A "hoax" classification for a real fire — because the system couldn't handle a caller who wasn't a calm, articulate adult on a voice line.

**Source:** UK Government case study, "Improving information about fire safety for deaf people in Cheshire," gov.uk.

---

## Story 6: The California Wave — Multiple cities, USA, 1986–87

**What happened:** After Mary Shufeldt's death, reports surfaced across California: a deaf woman in Riverside couldn't reach 911 during her husband's asthma attack; deaf roommates in Alameda trying to reach a suicidal friend had police investigate *them* for crank calls; a deaf man in Sonoma couldn't report a break-in. Investigation found many dispatch centers had TDD equipment connected to old numbers, not 911.

**Why it matters for Medak:** This wasn't one failure — it was systemic. And the fire chief's explanation? *"These people are such a small population, they tend to get lost in the shuffle."*

**Source:** Los Angeles Times, Feb 1, 1987.
