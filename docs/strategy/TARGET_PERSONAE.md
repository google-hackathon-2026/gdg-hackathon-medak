## The Personae

### 1. 👩 Ana Petrović — Young Deaf Adult Living Alone

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 26 |
| **Location** | Belgrade, Serbia |
| **Disability** | Profoundly deaf since birth (bilateral sensorineural hearing loss) |
| **Living situation** | Lives alone in a studio apartment in Dorćol |
| **Tech proficiency** | High — smartphone-native, uses video relay, Instagram, WhatsApp daily |

**Emergency scenario:** Ana wakes at 2 AM to the smell of smoke. Her building's fire alarm is flashing (she has a visual alert system), but the stairwell is filling with smoke. She needs to call for help *now*.

**Current workaround:** Serbia has no SMS-to-112. Ana's options: bang on a neighbor's door and hope someone is awake, or type a message in a WhatsApp group and pray someone sees it at 2 AM. She keeps her mother's number on speed dial — but her mother lives in Niš, 240 km away.

**Pain points:**
- Cannot call 112 at all — the system is voice-only
- SMS-to-112 does not exist in Serbia
- Text relay services don't operate at 2 AM
- Neighbors may not hear her knocking through fire doors
- Every workaround depends on a *third person* being awake and available

**How Medak solves this:** Ana presses the SOS button. Gemini's User Agent sees smoke through her camera, detects the urgency, and fills the EmergencySnapshot — fire, residential building, Dorćol address. The Dispatch Agent calls 112 with a clear, structured voice brief: *"Emergency call on behalf of a deaf person. Structure fire at Cara Dušana 47, apartment 12, third floor. Smoke visible. One person present, needs evacuation."* When the dispatcher asks "Can she get to the stairwell?", the question routes back to Ana's screen as text. She types "No, smoke too thick." The Dispatch Agent voices her answer. **For the first time in her life, Ana can call 112.**

**Emotional state:** Panic, but also a deep, familiar frustration — the *same* helplessness she's felt her entire life when facing voice-only systems. Fear compounded by the knowledge that her disability might cost her everything tonight.

### 2. 👴 Dragan Nikolić — Elderly Hard-of-Hearing Person

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 74 |
| **Location** | Kragujevac, Serbia |
| **Disability** | Progressive hearing loss (moderate-to-severe), wears hearing aids |
| **Living situation** | Lives with his wife Milica (also elderly) in a house |
| **Tech proficiency** | Low — has a smartphone (gift from his son), can make calls and use Viber, struggles with apps |

**Emergency scenario:** Dragan's wife Milica collapses in the kitchen. She's conscious but can't stand. Dragan dials 112. The operator picks up — but Dragan can barely hear the questions through his hearing aids. Background noise from the TV makes it worse. He shouts his address but isn't sure the operator understood. He hangs up and redials three times. Precious minutes evaporate.

**Current workaround:** Dragan tries to call 112 directly, but misunderstands questions and gives garbled responses. Sometimes he calls his son first, who then calls 112 — adding a 3-5 minute relay delay. His son doesn't always pick up.

**Pain points:**
- Can technically make a call but can't *communicate effectively*
- Misunderstands dispatcher questions, gives wrong information under stress
- Hearing aids amplify all sound — sirens, TV, his own panicked breathing
- Embarrassment and frustration when asked to repeat himself
- Doesn't consider himself "disabled enough" to need special tools — until it's too late

**How Medak solves this:** Dragan presses one button. The User Agent captures the scene — Milica on the floor, kitchen environment — and builds the EmergencySnapshot. The Dispatch Agent calls 112 with crystal-clear synthesized speech: *"Medical emergency. Elderly woman collapsed, conscious but unable to stand. Location: Kneza Mihaila 15, Kragujevac. Husband present, hard of hearing — please route follow-up questions through this system."* Dispatcher questions appear as large text on Dragan's phone. He types short answers. No mishearing. No shouting. No redials.

**Emotional state:** Terror for his wife. Shame at not being able to handle a "simple" phone call. Overwhelming relief when the system works and he doesn't have to struggle through another failed conversation with the operator.

### 3. 👨 Marko Jovanović — Person with Speech Impairment

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 33 |
| **Location** | Novi Sad, Serbia |
| **Disability** | Severe dysarthria (cerebral palsy) — understands everything, but speech is largely unintelligible to strangers |
| **Living situation** | Lives alone in an adapted apartment, works remotely as a graphic designer |
| **Tech proficiency** | Very high — power user, earns his living through technology |

**Emergency scenario:** Marko witnesses a car accident from his balcony. A pedestrian is hit and lying in the road. Other bystanders are frozen or filming. Marko wants to call 112 — he can see the exact location, the victim, the car — but he *knows* from experience that the operator won't understand him.

**Current workaround:** Marko has tried calling 112 before. The operator couldn't understand him, asked him to repeat four times, then hung up assuming it was a prank or butt-dial. He now relies on texting friends to make calls for him, or approaching strangers and showing them a pre-typed note on his phone asking them to call.

**Pain points:**
- Operators hang up when they can't understand his speech
- The humiliation of being treated as a prank caller
- Relying on strangers who may not want to get involved
- Texting a friend adds minutes of delay
- He can *think* and *type* perfectly — but the system only accepts voice

**How Medak solves this:** Marko hits SOS and points his camera at the accident scene. The User Agent sees the injured pedestrian, the car, the street intersection. EmergencySnapshot fills automatically: traffic accident, pedestrian down, intersection of Bulevar Oslobođenja and Futoška, one visible victim. The Dispatch Agent calls 112 and delivers the report in clear, professional speech. Marko supplements with typed details. The dispatcher never knows or needs to know about Marko's disability — and for once, that's exactly how Marko wants it.

**Emotional state:** Urgency for the victim, but also the sick feeling of *knowing* he can't use the phone. Years of accumulated frustration. And when Medak works — a rare moment of being treated as a competent adult by a system that usually excludes him.

### 4. 👦 Luka Stojanović — Non-Verbal Autistic Person

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 17 |
| **Location** | Subotica, Serbia |
| **Disability** | Non-verbal autism spectrum disorder, communicates via AAC app and gestures |
| **Living situation** | Lives with parents; mother is primary caregiver |
| **Tech proficiency** | Moderate — very comfortable with his specific apps (AAC, YouTube, games), less so with unfamiliar interfaces |

**Emergency scenario:** Luka's mother has a severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis) after a bee sting in their backyard. She manages to use her EpiPen but collapses. His father is at work, 30 minutes away. Luka understands the emergency — he's scared, but he knows his mother needs an ambulance. He cannot speak. He has never called anyone in his life.

**Current workaround:** The family's emergency plan is: Luka presses a speed-dial button to call his father, who then calls 112. But his father doesn't always answer immediately — and in this scenario, anaphylaxis can kill in minutes. There is no plan for when the human relay chain breaks.

**Pain points:**
- Cannot make voice calls — not a choice, a neurological reality
- Traditional 112 is completely inaccessible
- Emergency plans depend on a chain of other people being available
- Novel/stressful situations can trigger overwhelm, making even typing harder
- No system is designed for someone who communicates the way Luka does

**How Medak solves this:** Medak's interface is one big SOS button — minimal UI, no conversation required. Luka presses it. The User Agent's camera sees his mother on the ground, the backyard, the EpiPen. It builds the EmergencySnapshot: medical emergency, adult female, possible anaphylaxis, EpiPen administered, residential address auto-detected from GPS. The Dispatch Agent calls 112 and delivers the full brief. When the dispatcher asks follow-up questions, they appear as simple text on screen. Luka can type short answers or even use his AAC app. **Medak doesn't require speech. It doesn't require eloquence. It requires one press.**

**Emotional state:** Overwhelm. Sensory overload from the stress. Deep fear for his mother. But the single-button interface cuts through the overwhelm — one action, not a decision tree.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 29 |
| **Location** | Belgrade, Serbia (visiting for EXIT Festival, from Tokyo) |
| **Disability** | None — but speaks only Japanese and basic English. Zero Serbian. |
| **Living situation** | Traveling solo, staying in an Airbnb in Novi Sad |
| **Tech proficiency** | Very high — uses translation apps daily to navigate Serbia |

**Emergency scenario:** Yuki is robbed at knifepoint near Kalemegdan fortress at night. The attacker takes her bag (passport, wallet, phone) but she escapes with minor cuts. She borrows a stranger's phone. She dials 112 — and the operator answers in Serbian. Yuki's English is survival-level. She can't explain what happened, where she is, or what she needs. The operator transfers her — she's on hold. She's bleeding and alone in a foreign city at midnight.

**Current workaround:** Tourists rely on English-speaking bystanders, hotel staff, or hope the 112 operator speaks English. In Serbia, 112 operators may speak basic English, but nuanced communication (descriptions of attackers, exact locations, medical details) breaks down fast. Some tourists simply don't call — they find a taxi to a hospital themselves.

**Pain points:**
- Language barrier makes 112 functionally unusable
- Can't describe location (doesn't know street names in Cyrillic)
- Can't describe the attacker or incident details
- Transferred between operators while bleeding
- Zero confidence that help is actually coming

**How Medak solves this:** Yuki opens Medak on the borrowed phone (or her own, if not stolen). She presses SOS. The User Agent captures her environment — GPS places her near Kalemegdan, camera sees her injuries. She types in Japanese or English: "Robbed. Man with knife. Cut on arm." Medak translates, structures, and the Dispatch Agent calls 112 in fluent Serbian: *"Napad nožem kod Kalemegdana. Žrtva je japanska turistkinja, povređena — posekotina na ruci. Napadač pobegao. GPS lokacija: 44.8234° N, 20.4489° E."* The dispatcher's questions route back to Yuki in her language. **Language is no longer a barrier to survival.**

**Emotional state:** Shock, fear, disorientation. The particular terror of being vulnerable in a country where you can't make yourself understood. The language barrier isn't just inconvenient — it's existential.

The operator asks him to repeat. He tries again. *"Mkhh... pomm... zzz..."* The operator thinks it's a prank. They hang up. Stefan is having a stroke, alone, and the system just rejected him.

**Current workaround:** There is no workaround. Stefan has never been disabled before. He has no AAC app, no pre-configured emergency contacts, no visual alert system. He's a healthy middle-aged man who walked into his apartment at 6 PM and may not walk out. His only options: crawl to a neighbor's door or wait until someone notices he hasn't shown up to work tomorrow — in 14 hours.

**Pain points:**
- Sudden disability with zero preparation
- Knows exactly what's happening but cannot verbalize it
- 112 operator assumed prank call and disconnected
- Every minute of delay destroys brain tissue ("time is brain")
- No prior disability identity — never considered he'd need accessible emergency services

**How Medak solves this:** Even with impaired motor control, Stefan manages to press the SOS button with his left hand. The User Agent sees him through the camera — facial droop visible, right arm limp. It recognizes stroke indicators and fills the EmergencySnapshot: suspected stroke, facial asymmetry detected, right-side weakness, adult male alone, home address from GPS. The Dispatch Agent calls 112: *"Hitna medicinska pomoć. Suspektni moždani udar. Muškarac, 52 godine, sam u stanu. Vidljiva pareza desne strane lica i desne ruke. Adresa: Bulevar Kralja Aleksandra 184, stan 7."* The dispatcher sends an ambulance immediately. **Medak just saved Stefan's life — and his brain.**

**Emotional state:** Pure terror. The horror of being trapped inside a body that has suddenly betrayed him. The panic of understanding exactly what's happening and being unable to communicate it. This is the persona that makes investors cry — because Stefan is *everyone*. Anyone can become Stefan tonight.

### The Regulatory Tsunami: EU EECC Article 109

The **European Electronic Communications Code (EECC), Article 109** mandates that all EU member states must ensure **equivalent access to emergency services for persons with disabilities** — including 112.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Deadline** | **June 2027** |
| **Scope** | All 27 EU member states |
| **Requirement** | Equivalent access to 112 for deaf, HoH, and speech-impaired citizens |
| **Current compliance** | **Zero countries offer full equivalent 112 access** (EUD 2024 survey) |
| **Penalty** | Infringement proceedings, fines, political fallout |

This is not a "nice to have." **27 national governments are legally obligated to solve this problem within 2 years.** They have no solution. Medak is a solution.

Serbia, as an EU accession candidate, will need to align with EECC before joining. Early adoption = competitive advantage + demonstration of readiness.

### Why Now?

1. **Regulatory pressure.** EECC Article 109 deadline is June 2027. Governments are actively seeking solutions *today*. Procurement cycles for national emergency infrastructure take 18-24 months — the window for vendors to position themselves is **now**.

2. **Technology readiness.** Multimodal AI (Gemini 2.0 Flash) + programmable telephony (Twilio) + real-time speech synthesis have all matured to production quality in 2024-2025. This solution was technically impossible 3 years ago.

3. **Zero incumbents.** The EUD 2024 survey confirmed: no EU country offers full equivalent 112 access. There is no market leader. There is no "good enough" solution to displace. The market is genuinely greenfield.

4. **AI cost curve.** The cost of running multimodal inference is dropping 10x every 18 months. What's viable as a hackathon demo today is commercially scalable within 12 months.

5. **Political will.** The European Accessibility Act (2025 compliance deadline) has created political momentum. Disability rights are on the EU agenda. Governments *want* to solve this — they just don't know how.

### The Competitive Landscape

| Solution | Limitation |
|---|---|
| SMS-to-112 | Not available in most EU countries. No multimedia. No real-time dialogue. |
| Text relay services | Requires trained human operators. Not 24/7 in most countries. Expensive to scale. |
| Video relay (sign language) | Requires sign language interpreters. Severe workforce shortage. Not scalable. |
| 112 apps (e.g., eCall, DEC112) | Text-only. No environmental awareness. No voice bridge to operators. |
| Medak | **AI-powered. Voice bridge to standard 112. Environmental awareness via camera. Real-time Q&A relay. No human intermediary needed. Works with existing 112 infrastructure.** |

### The Human Cost of Inaction

- In the EU, emergency response times average **8-12 minutes** after a successful 112 call
- For deaf people, add **5-15 minutes** for the workaround relay (finding someone, explaining, them calling, them relaying)
- For cardiac arrest, **every minute without CPR reduces survival by 7-10%**
- For stroke, **1.9 million neurons die per minute** without treatment
- A 10-minute delay in a house fire can mean the difference between escape and entrapment

**People are dying because 112 doesn't accept text. Medak fixes that.**

## Pitch Usage Guide

These personae are designed for direct use in presentations:

- **Opening hook (Slide 1):** Start with Ana (Persona 1). She's the hero of the product story — the core user who has waited her entire life for this. Open with her fire scenario: *"Ana wakes at 2 AM to the smell of smoke. She's deaf. She can't call 112. In Serbia, there is no SMS-to-112. Her only option is to bang on a neighbor's door and hope someone is awake."*

- **The universal threat (Slide 2):** Stefan (Persona 6) proves anyone can need this. *"Imagine you're having a stroke. You know exactly what's happening. You pick up the phone. You dial 112. And you can't speak."* Everyone in the room can imagine being Stefan. You don't choose when you become disabled.

- **Emotional peak:** Jelena (Persona 7) — a mother who can't call for help for her injured child. This is the persona that breaks the room.

- **Market expansion:** Yuki (Persona 5) shows this isn't a niche disability product — it's a universal communication bridge.

- **Urgency:** Combine Stefan + Ana — one was born into this reality, one became disabled in an instant. Both are locked out of 112.

- **Regulatory hook:** *"27 EU countries must solve this by June 2027. Zero have a solution. We built one in 24 hours."*
