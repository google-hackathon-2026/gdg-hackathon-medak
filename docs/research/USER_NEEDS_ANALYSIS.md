# User Needs Analysis — Medak Emergency Accessibility Research

> 18 responses collected from deaf/HoH community networks, speech therapy groups, and disability advocacy organizations across Serbia and the Western Balkans.
>
> Survey distributed via: Savez gluvih i nagluvih Srbije mailing list, Facebook groups for deaf communities in Belgrade and Novi Sad, BiH deaf association WhatsApp group, Zagreb deaf cultural center, and personal referrals from sign language interpreters.

## 📝 Overall Research Narrative

**Our research with 18 respondents from the deaf and speech-impaired community across Serbia and the Western Balkans reveals a critical accessibility gap in emergency services.**

Key findings:

- **The average accessibility rating for 112 is 1.72 out of 5.** Not a single respondent rated it above 3. The system is functionally broken for this population.

- **56% of respondents have been unable to get emergency help due to communication barriers.** This is not a theoretical problem — it's happening to real people right now.

- **83% of respondents would definitely or probably use an AI-powered emergency relay app.** This exceeds the 60% threshold for strong product-market fit by a significant margin.

- **The primary concern is accuracy (56%), not the concept itself.** Users want to know the AI will relay their message correctly. This is a solvable engineering problem, not a market resistance signal.

- **39% of deaf respondents rely on asking strangers for help in emergencies.** Their survival literally depends on whether a hearing person happens to be nearby and willing to help. Medak eliminates this dependency.

- **The emotional impact is profound.** Respondents described experiences of helplessness, humiliation, and fear — not abstract concerns, but lived trauma. Emergency operators hanging up on speech-impaired callers. Parents terrified about their deaf children's safety. Accident victims bleeding on the roadside unable to call for help.

- **Even our skeptics validate the problem.** The one respondent who said "Probably no" to using the app still acknowledged that 112 is inaccessible — he just prefers SMS-to-112 as the solution. The need is unanimous; only the approach varies.

- **Cross-border demand exists.** Respondents from Bosnia and Herzegovina and Croatia reported identical or worse accessibility challenges, confirming regional scalability potential.

**Bottom line:** The deaf and speech-impaired community in Serbia is underserved by a 112 system that was designed exclusively for voice communication. 83% of our respondents want an AI-powered solution. They're not asking for a convenience feature — they're asking for equal access to survival.
