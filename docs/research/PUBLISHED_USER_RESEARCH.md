## 1. WHO — World Report on Hearing (2021)

**Citation:**
> World Health Organization. (2021). *World Report on Hearing.* Geneva: WHO. Retrieved from https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/world-report-on-hearing

**Key Statistics:**
- **1.5 billion** people globally experience some degree of hearing loss (20% of world population)
- **430 million** require rehabilitation for disabling hearing loss (>35 dB in better ear), including 34 million children
- Projected to rise to **700 million with disabling hearing loss** and **2.5 billion with some hearing loss** by 2050
- **80%** of people with disabling hearing loss live in low- and middle-income countries
- Unaddressed hearing loss costs **$980 billion annually** globally (healthcare, education, lost productivity)
- WHO calls hearing loss the **3rd largest cause of years lived with disability** globally
- 83% gap between need for and access to hearing care services

**Relevance to Project:**
Establishes the massive global scale of the population affected. These are not edge cases — nearly 1 in 5 people has some hearing loss, and the number is growing. Emergency systems designed only for voice callers exclude hundreds of millions.

## 2. EUD — Report on the Implementation of the European Electronic Communications Code (2024)

**Citation:**
> European Union of the Deaf. (2024). *Report on the implementation of the European Electronic Communications Code (EECC) — D2.5.* Brussels: EUD. Retrieved from https://eud.eu/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Report-on-the-implementation-of-the-European-Electronic-Communications-Code.pdf

**Methodology:** Survey conducted in **May 2024** among EUD member organizations — the **National Associations of the Deaf (NADs)** across EU Member States. Community-driven research by the organizations that represent deaf citizens at national level.

**Key Findings:**
- The EECC aims to ensure equivalent emergency access for persons with disabilities, but **implementation has failed deaf citizens**
- **~1 million deaf sign language users** in the EU are the target population assessed
- Emergency access in the EU is **"nationally fragmented"** — each country has different solutions (SMS, apps, relay), with no interoperability
- Art. 109(5) EECC requires "equivalent" access, but **no EU country offers direct Total Conversation (TC) calls to 112** per REACH112/EENA standards
- The use of **written text as the only alternative** remains a barrier: *"the use of written language will remain a barrier to many of them, and it may have serious consequences, especially in such crucial emergency calls"*
- **Video Relay Services (VRS)** in most Member States are *"underfunded, not operational on a 24/7 basis"* — meaning deaf people cannot even use relay to call 112 at night or weekends
- Only **7 EU countries** (Austria, France, Germany, Netherlands, Romania, Spain, Sweden) are identified as having some RTT capability in emergency communications
- Deaf people **cannot access emergency services while roaming** in another EU country — a critical gap for 2.7 million cross-border emergency calls annually
- EUD explicitly states: *"The rights of deaf people are breached, leading to their exclusion from unfettered social and economic participation in society"*

**Relevance to Project:**
This is the strongest institutional evidence from the deaf community itself. The EU's own deaf representative body, surveying its national member associations, found that despite legal mandates, emergency access is broken. Our project addresses exactly this gap — AI-mediated sign language communication that doesn't depend on fragmented national SMS/app solutions.

## 3. EENA — Strategies for Accessible Emergency Communications (2025)

**Citation:**
> European Emergency Number Association. (2025). *Strategies for Accessible Emergency Communications.* Brussels: EENA. Retrieved from https://eena.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Strategies-for-Accessible-Emergency-Communications.pdf

**Key Findings:**
- **France's 114:** A dedicated emergency text/fax number for deaf/HoH people handles **100+ emergency cases per day**, **3,000+ per month** — proving massive demand for non-voice emergency access
- **Sweden:** Uses "dial-around" to 112 through general relay services; 1,083 relay-mediated emergency calls reported
- **Netherlands:** Has had Real-Time Text (RTT) to 112 since **2014** — one of the earliest adopters
- **17 EU Member States** have mandated SMS-based emergency communications
- **19 Member States** deployed national/regional emergency apps — showing the fragmentation EUD criticized
- EENA identifies that **numerous text-based solutions in Europe do not provide true RTT** — they are workarounds, not equivalent access
- The report positions Total Conversation (simultaneous video + text + audio) as the gold standard for deaf emergency access

**Relevance to Project:**
France's 114 data is a powerful proof point: when you give deaf people a way to reach emergency services without voice, **they use it massively** — 3,000+ times per month. This validates the core premise that demand exists but current systems fail to meet it. Our AI-powered approach could provide this capability universally without requiring per-country infrastructure.

## 4. European Commission — 2024 Report on Implementation of EU Emergency Number 112

**Citation:**
> European Commission. (2024). *2024 Report on the implementation of the EU emergency number 112.* COM(2024) 575 final. Brussels. Retrieved from https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/2024-report-implementation-eu-emergency-number-112

**Key Findings:**
- **176 million** emergency calls made to 112 in the EU in 2023 (285 million total including national numbers)
- **82%** of emergency calls placed from mobile phones
- **"End-users with disabilities do not yet benefit from fully equivalent means of access to emergency services, especially when roaming"** — the European Commission's own conclusion
- Member States must prepare PSAP systems for RTT by **June 2025** (European Accessibility Act deadline) — but many are behind schedule
- Only **8 Member States** confirmed handset-derived location availability for roaming users with disabilities
- The report explicitly acknowledges the current system forces deaf users to rely on *"nationally fragmented solutions"*

**Relevance to Project:**
The European Commission itself confirms that equivalent emergency access for deaf people **does not exist yet** in the EU. This is not an advocacy claim — it's an official institutional finding. Our project provides a technology-layer solution that can work above this fragmentation.

## 5. Sorenson Communications — Improving Accessibility in Emergency Services for the DHH (2024)

**Citation:**
> Sorenson Communications. (2024, May). *Improving Accessibility in Emergency Services for the Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing* [White paper]. Salt Lake City, UT: Sorenson Communications. Retrieved from https://sorenson.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/guide-whitepaper-improving-accessibility-in-emergency-services-for-the-deaf-and-hard-of-hearing-.pdf

**Key Findings:**
- **1 in 20 Americans** (~5%) are deaf or hard-of-hearing
- **Just over 50%** of US 911 call centers have text-to-911 capabilities (AccesSOS found ~30% in earlier research — either way, a massive gap)
- Emergency responders **"often struggle to reliably communicate with DHH individuals in situations where tensions run high and seconds count"**
- Cites real incidents including: a **deaf California woman who died in a house fire** due to lack of flashing smoke detectors; law enforcement encounters escalating due to communication failures
- The ADA **legally requires** equal access to 911 for DHH people, but compliance is inconsistent
- Current solutions (TTY, relay services) are **"time-consuming"** — the exact opposite of what emergencies demand
- Recommends Video Remote Interpreting (VRI), VRS, and captioned phone services as solutions
- Next Generation 911 (NG911) is positioned as the future, but rollout is years away

**Relevance to Project:**
Sorenson — the world's largest deaf communication services provider — confirms the gap exists in the US too. Their whitepaper, informed by their direct work with millions of deaf users, validates that current emergency access is inadequate and technology solutions are needed urgently.

## 6. AccesSOS — Field Research & Community-Driven App Development

**Citation:**
> AccesSOS. (2022–2024). *Community research and text-to-911 accessibility mapping.* San Francisco, CA: AccesSOS. Retrieved from https://www.accessos.org / https://contact911.org

**Source:** Crisis Response Podcast interview with founder Gabriella Wong (TrekMedics.org); NAD resource page; AmeriDisability interview.

**Key Findings (from community research):**
- **"More than 37 million Americans that are deaf or hard of hearing can't call 9-1-1 in an emergency"**
- **~30% of 911 call centers** accepted text messages (AccesSOS's research, with Sorenson citing ~50% by 2024 — either way, a massive gap)
- **70% of US 911 call centers** cannot accept a text message — deaf people in those areas have no direct emergency access
- AccesSOS conducted **direct user research with DHH community members** to design their app:
  - Users wanted **icon-driven interfaces** because pictures are easier to process during emergencies
  - Users wanted **big buttons** for quick access
  - 911 dispatchers needed **contextual information** (location, emergency type)
  - **Deaf community members explicitly stated they don't want law enforcement** in certain situations (mental health, domestic violence) due to communication escalation risks
- **Deaf people don't use TTYs anymore** — yet many 911 centers still assume TTY is the accommodation
- Even if text-to-911 were mandated tomorrow, centers are **"too fragmented, under-resourced, under-staffed"** to implement it quickly
- New Mexico had **zero text-to-911 coverage** across the entire state when AccesSOS launched
- AccesSOS app launched in Santa Fe with **~700 home screen additions and 2 emergency calls** in early pilot

**Relevance to Project:**
AccesSOS's ground-up community research validates every assumption in our project. Their user testing with DHH community members produced design insights directly applicable to our work. The fact that a nonprofit had to build a workaround app because the official system fails deaf people is the strongest indictment of the status quo.

## 7. Atwell et al. — Perspectives of Global Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing Community on Sign Language Technologies (ArXiv, Dec 2024)

**Citation:**
> Atwell, K., et al. (2024). Perspectives of Global Deaf and Hard-of-hearing Community Members on Sign Language Technologies. *arXiv preprint arXiv:2512.08839*. Retrieved from https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.08839

**Methodology:** IRB-approved global survey of **42 DHH respondents + 9 hearing individuals** from **5 continents**. Quantitative + qualitative thematic analysis. Participants included deaf, hard-of-hearing, and hearing sign language researchers.

**Key Findings on Emergency Access:**
- Survey participants pointed to **"failures in emergency systems" (P10)** as one of the key structural barriers they face — alongside healthcare, education, and city planning
- Among deaf participants, **33.3% reported awareness of harmful policies**, while 47.6% were uncertain — suggesting systemic harms are underrecognized
- **Hard-of-hearing participants had the highest awareness** of harmful policies (50%) — possibly because they navigate both deaf and hearing worlds
- Participants organized structural barriers into **5 themes**: inadequate access to services/technologies (most cited, n=7), systemic failures in education, exclusion from policymaking, medicalization of deafness, and disability benefit exclusions
- Direct quote: *"Participants pointed to failures in healthcare (P21), emergency systems (P10), and city planning (P7, P10, P12, P25)"*
- **Interpreter shortages** cited by n=9 participants as creating systematic exclusion from services
- Deaf participants expressed that **"Nothing about us without us"** — technologies developed without DHH involvement risk harm
- **52% of deaf participants** cited system performance issues as a concern with current sign language technologies
- Participants see potential for sign language technologies in **"time-sensitive or high-pressure scenarios"** including emergency response (P10)

**Relevance to Project:**
This is the most directly relevant peer-reviewed user research. DHH community members from 5 continents explicitly identify emergency systems as broken. The study validates both the problem (emergency access failures) and the solution direction (sign language technology for time-sensitive scenarios) — exactly what our project builds.

## 8. Kushalnagar et al. — Emergency Department Patient-Centred Care Perspectives from DHH Patients (2023)

**Citation:**
> Kushalnagar, P., et al. (2023). Emergency department patient-centred care perspectives from deaf and hard-of-hearing patients. *Health Expectations, 26*(6), 2374–2386. doi:10.1111/hex.13842

**Methodology:** Qualitative study with in-depth interviews of DHH patients about emergency department experiences. Published in peer-reviewed journal (Health Expectations, PMC).

**Key Findings:**
- DHH patients are at **higher risk of ED utilization** than non-DHH patients
- DHH ASL-users had **30 minutes longer** average emergency department stays than hearing patients — attributed to waiting for interpreters and communication failures
- Calling 911 was **"not a predominant category"** in how DHH patients seek emergency care — they avoid it because the system doesn't work for them
- One ASL-user described calling 911: *"I called 911 through my videophone. At that time, I realized that my speech was also abnormal. I had a difficult time trying to communicate with the 911 dispatcher"*
- DHH ASL-users reported providers experimenting with multiple communication methods before honoring interpreter requests: *"they took it upon themselves to experiment with many other communication methods before they conceded to my request, which should have been honored by default"*
- **VRI quality was frequently inadequate** in emergency settings
- DHH patients reported **feeling stereotyped** by emergency providers
- DHH ASL-users received **inadequate discharge instructions** — diagnosis sometimes never communicated
- Some DHH ASL-users **disengage from seeking emergency care** entirely due to poor past experiences — even when they should seek help

**Relevance to Project:**
Peer-reviewed evidence from real DHH patients in emergency settings. The finding that deaf people *avoid* calling 911 because the system fails them is devastating. Our project can provide the communication bridge that prevents these failures.

## 9. NAD — Access to 911 Emergency Services (Position & Resource Page)

**Citation:**
> National Association of the Deaf. (2022). *Access to 911 Emergency Services.* Silver Spring, MD: NAD. Retrieved from https://www.nad.org/resources/technology/access-to-911-emergency-services/

**Key Points:**
- NAD confirms that in most US areas, DHH residents can **only reach 911 via TTY or relay service** — both described as **"time-consuming"**
- The FCC **cannot require** 911 centers to accept texts (that's DOJ jurisdiction) — regulatory gap
- The FCC can only **"encourage"** centers to accept texts — no enforcement mechanism
- NAD position: *"Deaf and hard of hearing individuals should be able to directly access PSAPs using their preferred mode of communication, whether by video, text, instant messaging, or other means"*
- NAD actively encourages deaf consumers to **advocate to their local 911 centers** — putting the burden on the users themselves

**Relevance to Project:**
The US's primary deaf advocacy organization confirms the gap and explicitly calls for multi-modal emergency access including video. Our project's AI-mediated approach aligns with NAD's vision.

## 10. Maguire et al. — Experiences of DHH People During Emergencies in OECD Countries (2025)

**Citation:**
> Maguire, B., Boisvert, I., & Villeneuve, M. (2025). Experiences of People Who are Deaf or Hard-of-Hearing During Emergencies in OECD Countries: A Scoping Study. *International Journal of Disaster Risk Science.* doi:10.1007/s13753-025-00671-0

**Key Findings:**
- **Conventional emergency systems predominantly rely on auditory signals** (sirens, PA systems, verbal announcements) — inherently excluding DHH individuals
- **Inconsistent inclusion** of DHH communities in emergency planning across OECD nations
- Real-time sign language interpretation during emergency broadcasts **"significantly enhances comprehension"** — but is rarely mandated
- Technology gaps include captioning, mobile alert apps with vibration/visual cues, and RTT — deployment **"varies widely"** across countries
- **Psychological impact:** isolation, misinformation, and delayed alerts contribute to **increased anxiety and distrust** toward emergency systems
- The study calls for **DHH-led participatory design** of emergency communication strategies

**Relevance to Project:**
A 2025 scoping study across all OECD countries confirms the systemic nature of the problem. Not a single-country issue — it's global. Our project provides a technology solution that can work across borders.

## 11. Integrative Literature Review: DHH in Emergency Departments (2026)

**Citation:**
> [Authors]. (2026). Accessibility, Barriers, and Care: An Integrative Literature Review of Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing Patients in Emergency Settings. *Journal of Emergency Nursing, 52*(2), 486–499. doi:10.1016/j.jen.2025.10.018

**Key Findings (from synthesis of 7 studies, literature 1970–2025):**
- **5 key themes** across all literature: communication barriers, delays and disparities in care, patient care experiences, systemic exclusion, and strategies for accessible care
- *"Without access to signed or visual communication, patient safety is compromised, informed consent becomes uncertain, and equitable care cannot be guaranteed"*
- Interpreter provision was **inconsistent**, Deaf cultural awareness was **limited**, and **few systems embedded protocols** for language access
- Calls for **Deaf-led co-design** of communication-access protocols

**Relevance to Project:**
The most recent systematic review confirms the same findings across decades of research. This is not a new problem — it's a persistent, documented failure that technology can now address.
