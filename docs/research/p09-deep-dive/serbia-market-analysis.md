## Executive Summary

Serbia presents a **uniquely compelling market opportunity** for an accessible emergency communications solution — but one that requires navigating a complex institutional landscape.

**Key findings:**

- **Serbia's unified 112 emergency number has been promised since 2011 but is STILL NOT operational** as of early 2026. Emergency services remain fragmented across 192 (police), 193 (fire), 194 (ambulance) — all voice-only.
- **~14,000 deaf and 70,000+ deaf/hard-of-hearing people in Serbia** have essentially **zero direct access to emergency services**. No SMS, no text, no relay, no app connection to PSAPs.
- A **"SOS for the Deaf" relay app** exists (launched ~2023) for general calls, but it routes through human sign language interpreters at the National Translation Centre — not directly to emergency PSAPs.
- Serbia's **EU accession process** mandates transposing the European Electronic Communications Code (EECC) and European Accessibility Act (EAA), both of which require accessible 112 access including Real-Time Text (RTT) and Total Conversation.
- The **Innovation Fund of Serbia's GovTech program** has already funded a similar project (TransportSign — sign language for railways), creating a clear precedent for our solution.
- **Multiple funding pathways exist**: IPA III, Innovation Fund GovTech, UNDP Serbia, OSCE Mission, and direct government procurement.

**Bottom line:** Serbia's combination of (a) an unfinished emergency system, (b) EU accession obligations mandating accessibility, and (c) an existing but inadequate relay service creates a perfect timing window for a solution that bridges the gap. The question isn't whether this will be needed — it's who builds it first.

## The Serbian 112 Emergency System

### Historical Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **2009** | EU begins observing European Day of 112 (Feb 11). Serbia notes obligation as part of EU accession process. |
| **2010** | Law on Emergency Situations adopted, mandating eventual introduction of 112. |
| **2011** | National Strategy on Protection and Rescue in Emergency Situations includes 112 implementation — but Action Plan never adopted. |
| **2012** | Deputy PM Ivica Dačić announces 112 will be operational "next year" (Feb 2012). It was not. |
| **2018** | Law on Disaster Risk Reduction and Emergency Management (Zakon o smanjenju rizika od katastrofa i upravljanju vanrednim situacijama) enacted. Defines "Service 112" (Služba 112) — early warning, notification, and alerting citizens. Still not implemented. |
| **2023 (Jul)** | Minister of Internal Affairs Bratislav Gašić announces 112 implementation to begin July 1, 2023. **Nothing happens.** MUP forms working group and drafts new National Strategy. Professor Cvetković estimates implementation needs "about a year" and "serious financial resources." |
| **2024** | MUP's Sector for Emergency Situations (Sektor za vanredne situacije) participates in 13 EU Civil Protection Mechanism deployments. Five UNDP-funded projects completed, eight ongoing — focused on disaster risk management. 112 system still not operational. |
| **2025-2026** | 112 remains a working draft. New National Strategy for Disaster Risk Reduction has been prepared with an Action Plan identifying implementation steps, but operational deployment has not occurred. |

**Key takeaway:** Serbia has been promising 112 for **14+ years** without delivering. The system exists in law (since 2018) but not in practice. Every emergency service still operates its own dispatch center with its own number.

### Current Emergency Numbers

| Number | Service | Operator | Accessibility |
|--------|---------|----------|---------------|
| **192** | Police (Policija) | MUP | Voice only |
| **193** | Fire/Rescue (Vatrogasci) | MUP - Sektor za vanredne situacije | Voice only |
| **194** | Ambulance (Hitna pomoć) | Ministry of Health | Voice only |
| **112** | Unified emergency | MUP (planned) | **NOT OPERATIONAL** |

**None of these numbers accept SMS, text, or any non-voice communication.** Deaf people in Serbia literally cannot call for emergency help directly.

### PSAP Infrastructure

Serbia does **not** have traditional PSAPs in the EU sense. Instead:

- **MUP's Sector for Emergency Situations** (Sektor za vanredne situacije) is the designated body for 112. It operates from four regional headquarters:
  1. **Belgrade** (Uprava za vanredne situacije u Beogradu)
  2. **Novi Sad** (Uprava za vanredne situacije u Novom Sadu)
  3. **Niš** (Uprava za vanredne situacije u Nišu)
  4. **Kragujevac** (Uprava za vanredne situacije u Kragujevcu)
- Plus additional departmental offices (Odeljenja za vanredne situacije) at the local level.
- Each emergency service (police, fire, ambulance) currently operates its **own independent dispatch center**.

**The planned 112 system** would create a unified intake that routes to the appropriate service. This architecture is still on paper.

### Technology Stack

- **Current:** Legacy voice telephony. Each dispatch center uses its own systems. No unified CAD (Computer-Aided Dispatch), no integrated GIS, no AML (Advanced Mobile Location).
- **NG112 status:** Not on the near-term roadmap. Serbia needs to first implement basic 112 before considering NG112. The OSCE's comparative review notes Serbia should "create all prerequisites for the full establishment and functioning of European unified emergency number 112 including development and adoption of an Ordinance on the single European emergency number."
- **Chinese/Huawei involvement:** Serbia has received various Chinese technology donations for surveillance and smart city infrastructure. While there's no confirmed Huawei 112 system, the government's orientation toward Chinese tech could influence procurement choices.

### MUP's Role

The **Ministry of Internal Affairs (Ministarstvo unutrašnjih poslova / MUP)** is the designated authority for 112 infrastructure. Specifically:

- The **Sector for Emergency Situations** (one of MUP's main organizational units) is tasked with establishing the 112 system.
- MUP's Sector has ~3,300 specialized fire and rescue units and emergency first responders.
- MUP coordinates with the EU Civil Protection Mechanism (activated 13 times in 2024 for international aid).
- The Sector handles early warning, notification, alerting, and coordination during emergencies.

**Political context:** MUP is one of Serbia's most powerful ministries. Any 112 solution requires MUP's blessing and participation.

### Serbian Sign Language (Srpski znakovni jezik)

- **Legal status:** The **Law on the Use of Sign Language** (Zakon o upotrebi znakovnog jezika) was adopted in **2015**.
- **Critical gap:** The law promotes access to learning sign language and interpreter services, but **does NOT explicitly recognize "Serbian Sign Language"** as a distinct language, nor does it recognize deaf culture. (Source: UNPRPD Situational Analysis 2024)
- **Deaf-blind people** are also overlooked — different interpretation services for this group are not recognized.
- **Interpreter shortage:** There is no comprehensive or formal education/training program for Serbian Sign Language interpreters in Serbia. The profession lacks formal recognition.
- **Linguistic reality:** Serbian Sign Language is the mother tongue of deaf people in Serbia. Serbian (written/spoken) is their second language. As stated by the National Translation Centre: "Deaf people in Serbia still need to explain that Serbian is not their mother tongue and that textual translation from spoken to written Serbian does not mean much to them."

**This is a critical design consideration:** Text-based solutions alone (SMS, chat) are not fully accessible. Solutions must include video/sign language capability to be truly equivalent.

### Technology Usage

Serbian deaf people primarily use:
- **Smartphones** with video calling (Viber, WhatsApp dominant in Serbia)
- **SOS for the Deaf app** (SOS za gluve) — video relay through interpreters
- **National Translation Centre for Serbian Sign Language** — 24/7 video relay service since September 2021
- **Written SMS/chat** — limited effectiveness due to Serbian being a second language for deaf users

### Existing Relay Services

**National Centre for Serbian Sign Language Interpreting** (Nacionalni centar za prevođenje srpskog znakovnog jezika):
- **Opened:** September 23, 2021 (International Day of Sign Languages)
- **Hours:** 24/7
- **How it works:** Deaf person makes video call → sign language interpreter answers → interpreter calls the hearing party by phone → simultaneous interpretation
- **Funding:** Initial RSD 16.2 million (~€138,000) donation from Japan Tobacco International (JTI), through Serbian Philanthropy Forum, via USAID-supported National Giving Day
- **Operators:** Belgrade City Organization of the Deaf + Association of Deaf and HoH of Serbia + Association of Young Deaf People of Serbia
- **Scale:** Serves 70,000+ users across Serbia

**SOS for the Deaf App** (SOS za gluve):
- **Launched:** ~2023, presented with Prime Minister Ana Brnabić
- **Platform:** Android (Google Play), developed in cooperation with Office for IT and eGovernment
- **Features:** Video calls with interpreters, chat with interpreter, emergency calls (routed through interpreter), appointment scheduling, contact management
- **Telecom support:** Serbian telecom companies provide free data/calls for app usage
- **Minister Mihailo Jovanović (IT and Telecom)** stated the app "enables video calls, chat with an interpreter and emergency calls"

**Critical gap:** While the app mentions "emergency calls," these go through the interpreter relay — the interpreter then calls the emergency number (192/193/194) by voice. This introduces:
- **Delay:** Adding an interpreter intermediary
- **Availability:** Depends on interpreter availability at the National Translation Centre
- **No direct PSAP integration:** Emergency dispatchers don't see text, location, or video
- **No AML/location data:** The relay call doesn't carry automatic caller location

**UN Women Serbia (2022)** project also deployed tablets in all 66 basic courts in Serbia for video relay access to sign language interpreters — showing institutional adoption of the relay model for justice services.

### Documented Incidents

1. **1999 NATO Bombing:** "A family from Užice, in which all members were deaf, was not aware that the bombing had started until someone left a message in their mailbox **days after**." — Aneta Eminagić Duraković, Association of the Deaf and HoH, speaking at OSCE conference. (Source: OSCE Mission to Serbia, 2020)

2. **2014 Floods:** "It is important that we don't get information late, as we did during the floods in 2014." — Mihailo Gordić, Association of the Deaf and HoH. (Source: OSCE Mission to Serbia, 2020)

3. **Systemic issue:** Less than 3% of content on RTS1 (Serbian public broadcaster) is accessible to people with disabilities. Private TV stations with national coverage have **zero** adapted programming. (Source: OSCE Mission to Serbia, 2020)

4. **Conference conclusion (2020):** The OSCE-supported conference on "People with Disabilities in Emergency Situations" concluded there is "a need for introducing a **national call center and mobile application** able to provide early warning information in real-time in different formats: audio, video, and in sign language." — Mihailo Gordić. (Source: OSCE Mission to Serbia, 2020)

| AML (Advanced Mobile Location) | ❌ Not implemented | — |

| RTT (Real-Time Text) to PSAP | ❌ Not available | Required by EU for accession |
| Total Conversation | ❌ Not available | Required by EU for accession |

### The Gap

The gap is **massive and clear:**

1. **Direct gap:** Deaf people cannot directly contact emergency services at all. The only path is through a human interpreter relay, which adds latency, depends on interpreter availability, and loses location data.

2. **Systemic gap:** Even hearing people don't have a unified 112 — they must know which number to call (192/193/194) depending on the emergency type.

3. **Technology gap:** Serbian PSAPs have no capability to receive text, video, or data communications. They are pure voice.

4. **EU compliance gap:** As Serbia progresses toward EU membership, it will need to implement RTT and Total Conversation per the EECC and EAA. There is currently zero infrastructure for this.

## Serbian Legal Framework

### Key Laws and Their Provisions

#### 1. Law on the Use of Sign Language (2015)
- Promotes access to sign language learning and interpreter services
- **Does NOT** explicitly recognize "Serbian Sign Language" as a distinct language
- **Does NOT** recognize deaf culture
- **Does NOT** address emergency access specifically
- No provisions for interpreter training/certification

#### 2. Law on Disaster Risk Reduction and Emergency Management (2018)
- Defines "Service 112" (Služba 112) for early warning, notification, and alerting
- Mandates establishment of 112 — but implementation has not occurred
- **No specific provisions for accessibility** of emergency communications for persons with disabilities

#### 3. Law on Prevention of Discrimination Against Persons with Disabilities (2006)
- General anti-discrimination framework
- Covers access to services but not specifically emergency communications
- Serbia ratified the **UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** on December 17, 2007

#### 4. Law on Electronic Communications
- Governs telecom operators and services
- Serbia is in the process of harmonizing with the **EU Electronic Communications Code (EECC - Directive 2018/1972)**
- The EECC mandates (Article 109): Member States must ensure "equivalent access" to emergency services for persons with disabilities, "where feasible without any pre-registration"

#### 5. Law on Electronic Government (2018)
- Establishes framework for e-government services
- Supports digitalization of public services
- The Office for IT and eGovernment operates under this law

### EU Accession Obligations

Serbia opened EU accession negotiations and must align its legislation with the EU acquis. The most relevant directives are:

| EU Directive/Regulation | Requirement | Serbia Status |
|------------------------|-------------|---------------|
| **EECC** (Directive 2018/1972) Art. 109 | Equivalent emergency access for persons with disabilities; RTT implementation; caller location | Not transposed |
| **European Accessibility Act** (Directive 2019/882) | Emergency services must respond with same communication method received (text→text, video→video); free of charge | Not transposed |
| **Delegated Regulation 2023/444** | Functional equivalence requirements: two-way interactive communication, no pre-registration, free, routed to qualified PSAP, equivalent location, awareness campaigns | Not transposed |
| **RTT mandate** | EU deadline: Native RTT by June 28, 2025 (or derogation to June 28, 2027); Total Conversation where available | Not applicable yet (Serbia not EU member) but will be required for accession |

**Strategic implication:** Serbia WILL need to implement these requirements as part of EU accession. The Chapter 10 (Information Society and Media) negotiations will require alignment with the EECC. This creates a **guaranteed future demand** for the solution we're building.

### Government Initiatives for Accessibility

1. **OSCE Mission to Serbia** — supported conference on "People with Disabilities in Emergency Situations" (2020); produced Family Guide in Braille; audio version of emergency guide
2. **UNDP Serbia** — 5 completed + 8 ongoing projects on disaster risk management, funded primarily by EU
3. **UN Women Serbia** — deployed tablets in 66 courts for video relay (2022)
4. **SOS for the Deaf app** — developed with Office for IT and eGovernment cooperation

## Go-to-Market Strategy for Serbia

### Strategic Positioning

**Don't sell technology. Sell EU compliance.**

Serbia's government is laser-focused on EU accession. Frame the solution as:
> "The platform that makes Serbia's emergency services compliant with the European Electronic Communications Code and European Accessibility Act — before EU membership requires it."

This transforms the conversation from "nice to have" accessibility to "must have" EU compliance.

### Target Stakeholders (Priority Order)

1. **Office for IT and eGovernment** — Already built the SOS for the Deaf app. Understands the problem. Reports to PM. This is your champion.
2. **Belgrade City Organization of the Deaf** (Mihailo Gordić) — Operates the National Translation Centre and SOS app. Will validate the solution and provide community access.
3. **MUP - Sector for Emergency Situations** — The 112 authority. Needs to be involved but may be slower to engage. Approach via UNDP Serbia (existing relationship).
4. **Innovation Fund of Serbia** — GovTech funding. Apply with #1 or #3 as the public sector partner.
5. **UNDP Serbia** — Implementation partner. Can fund through EU IPA channels.
