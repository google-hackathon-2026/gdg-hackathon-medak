## 1. Real Stories: When Seconds Became Hours

### 1.1 Mary Shufeldt — San Diego, USA (1986)

**The story that started a national conversation.**

Jay Shufeldt, a retired deaf newspaper printer, came home to find his 72-year-old wife Mary struggling to breathe. He did what anyone would do — he called 911. Using his TDD (telecommunications device for the deaf), he dialed emergency services.

**The 911 operator hung up.** They mistook the high-pitched beeping of the TDD device for a child playing with a touch-tone phone.

Over the **next three hours**, Jay called 911 **three times**. Each time — no response. He watched his wife deteriorate. Her mouth moved slowly. Her lips grew parched. He couldn't leave her. She was too heavy to carry to his car. His neighbors spoke only Spanish and had no phone.

Finally, Jay reached his daughter, who could hear. She called 911 "by voice." Paramedics arrived. They tried resuscitation. Then they shook their heads and placed Mary back on the bed.

**Mary Shufeldt died of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease** — a condition that, according to a pulmonary specialist, was treatable with prompt emergency care. *"It's the kind of problem where if emergency personnel arrive at the scene and assist the person, it can be handled."*

Two weeks later, her attorney called 911 from his office TDD while a police investigator watched. **The 911 office hung up on him three times in a row.**

*"I explained to him that those three calls could easily have represented three separate life-threatening emergencies for different deaf people. And he didn't have any explanation for it."* — Attorney Greg Relyea

> **Source:** Los Angeles Times, "A Deadly Silence: 911 Calls Weren't the Answer for Mute Man Seeking Help for Dying Wife," August 25, 1986; "Deaf Confront Deadly Problem: Unanswered Help Calls to 911," February 1, 1987.

---

### 1.2 The California Wave — Multiple Cases (1986–1987)

Mary Shufeldt's death wasn't isolated. It triggered a cascade of reports:

- **Riverside County:** A deaf woman couldn't reach 911 when her husband had a severe asthma attack. She had to contact a deaf friend whose hearing son called for help.
- **Alameda County:** Deaf roommates couldn't reach 911 when they believed a friend was suicidal. After they broke down his door and took him to the hospital, police arrived — to investigate what they assumed were *crank calls*.
- **Sacramento:** A deaf man couldn't reach 911 after witnessing a car hit a child. Another couldn't get through after suffering a stroke at a party. In both cases, neighbors eventually called.
- **Sonoma County:** A deaf man tried calling 911 believing someone was breaking into his home. He failed. Police later admitted an inexperienced operator hadn't understood the TDD.

Fire Chief Ron Coleman of Fullerton investigated and found that **many emergency dispatch centers had their TDD equipment hooked up to old seven-digit numbers** rather than the 911 system. Many TDDs were in disrepair because they received so few calls.

*"What it boils down to is these people are such a small population, they tend to get lost in the shuffle."* — Fire Chief Ron Coleman, Fullerton

> **Source:** Los Angeles Times, February 1, 1987.

### 1.3 Gabriella Wong's Father — The AccesSOS Origin Story (2010s)

Gabriella Wong grew up communicating with two deaf parents in American Sign Language. When her father William was in a **car accident**, he didn't call 911. He couldn't. Instead, he **texted his daughter** to ask her to call 911 for him.

The next time was worse. William's **gallbladder ruptured**. He was alone. He texted Gabriella for help — but she didn't see the messages in time.

*"During one of the most vulnerable, desperate moments of his life, he couldn't contact 911 to get help. He almost died because of this inequity."* — Gabriella Wong, Founder of AccesSOS

William survived. Gabriella went on to found AccesSOS, a nonprofit making emergency services accessible to deaf Americans. As of 2025, **37 million deaf or hard-of-hearing Americans** and **29.6 million with limited English proficiency** cannot reliably call 911.

> **Source:** AccesSOS website (accessos.io); AmeriDisability interview; Stand Together profile.

### 1.4 The Cheshire Fire Case — UK

A young person with two deaf parents called 999 to report a fire in their home. The child was **unsure what to say** and felt deeply uncomfortable speaking to the call handler. Overwhelmed, the child **hung up** before giving any information beyond the address.

**The call was categorized as a hoax.** No fire engine was sent.

The father — deaf — managed to directly contact his local fire station. A fire engine was eventually dispatched. The family survived without injury.

This incident prompted Cheshire Fire and Rescue Service to hold deaf awareness events and introduce text-based emergency access. Simple changes. But it took a near-disaster to make them happen.

> **Source:** UK Government Case Study, "Improving information about fire safety for deaf people in Cheshire," gov.uk.

### 1.5 Andrew Jordan — Car Crash, London, UK (2013)

Andrew Jordan, who is deaf, and his family were struck by a drunk driver near Belvedere, Bexley. His wife (also deaf) suffered head and facial injuries. Two toddlers were in the car.

**Not a single first responder** — police, fire, or ambulance — could communicate with Andrew or his wife.

Andrew, despite being injured himself, had to **write notes and relay in sign language** to his wife what was happening. He asked paramedics to call ahead to Queen Elizabeth Hospital to arrange a BSL interpreter. At the hospital, despite this request, **no interpreter was arranged** and no one knew how to get one. Andrew had to call a hearing friend to come translate.

The police officer at the scene told Andrew — a deaf man, with an injured wife and two toddlers, at a crash caused by a drunk driver — that **he had to arrange his own car towing**. She gave him a phone number. Not a text number. Not an email. A phone number.

*"Being involved in any car accident is a stressful and worrying ordeal, but… this is another example of a case when the emergency services seem ill equipped to deal with deaf people effectively."* — Deaf Magazine, December 2013

> **Source:** Deaf Magazine UK, "Deaf Man Fails to Get the Support He Needs Following Accident," December 29, 2013.

### 1.6 Magdiel Sanchez — Fatal Police Encounter, Oklahoma City, USA (2017)

Magdiel Sanchez was deaf. When police arrived at his home regarding a hit-and-run investigation, Sanchez came out holding a metal pipe (he was known to carry one as a walking aid). Neighbors screamed at police: **"He can't hear you! He's deaf!"**

Officers ordered him to drop the weapon. He couldn't hear them. An officer deployed a Taser. Another simultaneously fired his gun.

**Magdiel Sanchez was killed.**

While not directly about calling 911, this case illustrates the lethal consequences of emergency systems and responders who are not equipped to communicate with deaf individuals. The communication failure was total, from first contact to the final moment.

> **Source:** Multiple news reports, 2017. Oklahoma City Police Department shooting of Magdiel Sanchez.

---

### 1.7 UK Emergency Services — Systemic Patterns

Research by RNID (Royal National Institute for Deaf People) and SignHealth in the UK found:

- **Almost 1 in 10** deaf or hard-of-hearing people have **avoided calling an ambulance or visiting A&E** because of communication barriers.
- **1 in 4** have **delayed seeking help** for new health concerns.
- British Deaf Association (2019): Many police officers knew how to book foreign language interpreters but **had no idea how to arrange a BSL interpreter**.
- Sheffield Hallam University (2020): Access to qualified BSL interpreters in police stations **remains inconsistent**, despite legal protections.

> **Source:** Emergency Services Times, "Deaf Unity helps first responders break down communication barriers," September 2025; RNID/SignHealth joint findings.

## 2. The Balkans & Serbia Context

### 2.1 Serbia's Deaf Population

- Serbia has approximately **50,000–60,000 deaf and hard-of-hearing citizens** (estimated based on WHO prevalence rates applied to Serbia's ~6.6M population).
- The **Savez Gluvih i Nagluvih Srbije** (Association of the Deaf and Hard of Hearing of Serbia) is the primary advocacy organization.
- Serbian Sign Language was recognized by Serbia's government but comprehensive accessibility legislation trails behind EU standards.

### 2.2 Emergency Access in Serbia

Serbia's 112 emergency system is **voice-only.** There is:

- ❌ No SMS-to-112 service
- ❌ No text relay service
- ❌ No emergency app with accessibility features
- ❌ No video relay service for sign language
- ❌ No total conversation capability

According to EENA's accessibility table, **Serbia is not listed** among countries with SMS emergency access — placing it alongside only a handful of European nations without any alternative communication channel to 112.

For a deaf person in Belgrade experiencing a cardiac emergency alone at home, the current options are:

1. Text a hearing friend or family member and hope they see it in time
2. Go to a neighbor's door and hope they're home and understand
3. Try to physically get to a hospital

There is no direct path from a deaf person's phone to emergency services. **None.**

### 2.3 Regional Comparison

| Country | SMS to 112? | App? | Video Relay? |
|---------|------------|------|-------------|
| **Serbia** | ❌ No | ❌ No | ❌ No |
| Croatia | ✅ Yes | ❌ No | ❌ No |
| Slovenia | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes (WAP 112) | ❌ No |
| Romania | ✅ Yes | ❌ No | ❌ No |
| Bulgaria | ❌ No (tender opened 2018) | ❌ No | ❌ No |

Serbia's deaf citizens have **zero equivalent access** to emergency services. This isn't a gap — it's a wall.

> **Source:** EENA Accessibility Table (2018); EENA Emergency Services Accessibility Report (2021 Update).

### 3.3 The Time That Kills

Emergency medicine operates on brutal math:

| Time Factor | Impact | Source |
|-------------|--------|--------|
| Cardiac arrest survival drops | **7–10% for every minute** without defibrillation | AHA / Emergency Medicine literature |
| After 10 minutes without CPR/defibrillation | Survival rate approaches **near zero** | AHA Guidelines |
| Average 911 call to dispatch | **~1 minute** | NENA data |
| SMS emergency conversation duration | **10–18 minutes** | EENA Report |
| Deaf person's typical emergency contact chain | **Minutes to hours** (text friend → friend calls → dispatch → response) | Case studies above |

**The math is devastating.** A hearing person calls 112 → ambulance dispatched in ~60 seconds → arrives in 8-12 minutes. Total: ~10-13 minutes.

A deaf person texts a friend → friend sees the text (minutes? hours?) → friend calls 112 → dispatch → response. Total: **unpredictable, potentially fatal.**

In Mary Shufeldt's case: **3 hours.** She died.

In Gabriella Wong's father's case: he survived only because his gallbladder rupture was slow enough to not kill him in the time it took his daughter to see his text.

### 3.4 The Mortality Gap

While no single study quantifies exact mortality differentials for deaf people in emergencies (a research gap that itself tells a story), the evidence is clear from emergency medicine:

- **Every 1-minute delay** in cardiac arrest response reduces survival by 7-10%
- **Stroke:** "Time is brain" — 1.9 million neurons die every minute during a stroke (AHA)
- **Trauma:** The "golden hour" concept — survival rates drop significantly after 60 minutes
- A deaf person's emergency response chain adds **at minimum 5-15 minutes** and potentially hours of delay
- For cardiac arrest, even a 5-minute delay means survival drops from ~50% to under 20%

## 4. The Psychological Burden

### 4.1 Living with the Knowledge

Imagine knowing — every single day — that if you have a heart attack, if your house catches fire, if someone breaks in, you cannot call for help. Not "it will be harder." Not "it will take longer." **You cannot do it at all.**

This is the daily reality for deaf people in countries without accessible emergency services.

Research findings on the psychological impact:

- **Chronic hypervigilance:** Deaf individuals report heightened anxiety about being alone, traveling to unfamiliar places, or being in situations where emergencies might occur.
- **Avoidance behavior:** 1 in 10 deaf people in the UK have avoided emergency situations entirely — not going to certain places, not doing certain activities — because of the fear that they couldn't get help if something went wrong. (RNID/SignHealth)
- **Dependency anxiety:** Many deaf people describe feeling forced to depend on hearing friends or family for their basic safety, creating feelings of inadequacy, burden, and loss of independence.
- **Compounding isolation:** Deaf people already face social isolation; the knowledge that emergency systems exclude them reinforces the message that society wasn't built for them.
- **Trauma from past failures:** Those who have experienced emergency communication breakdowns (like Jay Shufeldt, like Andrew Jordan) carry lasting trauma from those moments.

### 4.2 A Quote from the Community

*"People with disabilities are discriminated against while accessing emergency services."* — EENA REACH112 Report, submitted to the European Parliament

*"For deaf people, [voice-only 112] is not accessible as this means they are unable to use their native languages to communicate in emergency situations which is of particular importance when in urgent and pressurised circumstances."* — European Union of the Deaf (EUD), 2024

*"These people are such a small population, they tend to get lost in the shuffle."* — Fire Chief Ron Coleman, Fullerton, California, 1987

That last quote was from **1987**. Nearly 40 years later, the problem persists.

— EENA REACH112 Report to the European Parliament

## 6. Hackathon Pitch Narrative — "The Silent Emergency"

### 🎬 The Opening Story (2 minutes)

*[Read slowly. Let silences land.]*

---

**"Meet Ana."**

Ana is 34. She lives alone in a small apartment in Belgrade. She's a graphic designer. She has friends, a cat named Miki, and a life she's built with pride.

Ana is deaf.

Last Tuesday at 2 AM, Ana woke up to a sharp pain in her chest. Her left arm went numb. Her heart was racing. She knew the signs — she'd read about them many times, the way deaf people read about a lot of things that could go wrong.

She reached for her phone.

And then she froze.

Because in Serbia, the emergency number is 112. And 112 is a phone call. A voice call. You dial. You speak. Someone listens. Someone sends help.

Ana can't speak on the phone. She can't hear the dispatcher. There is no text option. No app. No video relay. No SMS service. **Nothing.**

*[Pause]*

So Ana did what deaf people have been doing for decades. She texted her friend Marija. "Mislim da imam srčani udar. Pozovi hitnu." *I think I'm having a heart attack. Call the ambulance.*

It was 2 AM. Marija was asleep.

Ana waited. One minute. Three minutes. Five minutes.

In those five minutes, if Ana was having a cardiac arrest, her chance of survival dropped from 50% to under 20%.

At seven minutes, Marija woke up. Saw the text. Called 112. Explained the situation. Gave Ana's address.

The ambulance was dispatched. It arrived 12 minutes later.

**From the moment Ana felt chest pain to the moment help arrived: 24 minutes.**

For a hearing person making the same call? **About 10 minutes.**

Ana was lucky. Her friend woke up. It wasn't a full cardiac arrest. She survived.

**But what about next time?**

What about the deaf woman whose friend's phone is on silent? The deaf man who lives alone and has no one to text at 3 AM? The deaf child whose parents can't call for help when they're the ones who are hurt?

Right now, in 2026, in Serbia, a deaf person having a heart attack has **no way** to directly reach emergency services from their phone.

Not a slow way. Not a difficult way. **No way.**

*[Pause]*

**What if their phone could call for them?**

### 🎯 Pitch Delivery Notes

- **Do not rush.** The pauses are as important as the words.
- **"Ana" should feel like someone the judges might know.** Not a statistic. A person.
- The story is a composite based on real patterns documented in every case study above. The Mary Shufeldt case (3 hours, death) is the extreme. Ana's story is the *moderate* version — and it's still terrifying.
- After "What if their phone could call for them?" — transition immediately to the solution. Don't let the emotion dissipate.
- **Emotional but not exploitative:** Ana survives. The story ends with possibility, not tragedy. The tragedy is in the "what about next time?" — it's a question, not a death.
